Sinead O'Connor's cause of death revealed after Irish music legend passed away aged 56 in July last

Publish date: 2024-02-21

Sinéad O'Connor died of natural causes, a coroner announced today.

The star, 56, was found 'unresponsive' after police were called to her flat in Herne Hill, south-east London, in July last year. 

Detectives did not not treat the star's sudden death as suspicious and today officials said she had died of natural causes. 

'This is to confirm that Ms O'Connor died of natural causes,' a spokesman for Southwark Coroners Court told MailOnline. 'The coroner has therefore ceased their involvement in her death.' 

The news comes after details of the line up was revealed for a tribute concert for her and Shane MacGowan, who also died recently. Artists including cat Power, David Gray, Dropkick Murphys and Glen Hansard will perform on March 20 in New York's legendary Carnegie Hall.

What does 'natural causes' actually mean? 

Many associate the phrase 'natural causes' with old age, but it simply signals that a death was the result of illness within the body, rather than being caused by an external factor like a car accident.

A natural cause of death is confirmed by a coroner's certificate, which states that a post-mortem is not needed, based on a doctor's medical certificate.

Dr Kathryn Pinneri, pathologist and president of the National Association of Medical Examiners in the UK, said deaths caused by cancer, stroke or diabetes would be classed as 'natural causes'. Examples of 'non-natural' deaths include road traffic accidents, suicide, drug overdoses and accidents such as drowning, she told HuffPost.

And Dr David Fowler, an anatomic and forensic pathologist, described the manner of death to CNN as dying from a 'natural disease process'.

He said examples of these are illnesses that 'are going to carry us off at some point along the way', such as infections or heart disease.

'If I'm playing a sport and have a heart attack… or shovel snow and have a heart attack because I stressed myself, that's natural,' Dr Fowler said. 'But if a person playing sport was to die from something caused by the activity, such as a head injury, that death would not be ''natural''. There is a difference between the ''cause'' and ''manner'' of death.

And despite the fact the term is death by 'natural causes', this is actually the 'manner' of death - the determination of how the injury or disease leads to death. The coroner rules whether the manner of death was natural, accidental, or due to suicide or homicide. Whereas the 'cause' is the disease or injury itself that leads to death, such as stroke.
