The true story behind 'Oppenheimer', starring Cillian Murphy

Publish date: 2024-07-02

This year's awards season has seen plenty of standout moments, from Lily Gladstone's historic win at the Golden Globes, to Taylor Swift's surprise new album announcement onstage at the Grammys. Last night at the BAFTAs, however, it was one film in particular that dominated the headlines: the standout hit of summer 2023, Oppenheimer, starring Cillian Murphy in the title role.

The epic – which scored five Golden Globes earlier this year – was the biggest winner of the night and took home seven awards, including Best Film. Murphy (the first Irish star to ever win the Best Actor award) thanked his "Oppenhomies" as he accepted his gong, describing his character as "colossally knotty" and adding: "We have a space to debate and interrogate and investigate that complexity and it's a privilege to be a part of this community with you all."

Based on the life of American theoretical physicist J. Robert Oppenheimer, the eponymous film explores his invention of the atomic bomb – and the implications of creating such a deadly weapon. But who was the real Robert Oppenheimer? Here, we explore the true story behind the film.

Who was the real J. Robert Oppenheimer?

Born in New York in 1904, Oppenheimer – played by Cillian Murphy in the new film – majored in chemistry at Harvard and then went on to study at Cambridge. A tall, thin chain smoker, he was described by many friends as self-destructive, and was prone to periods of depression throughout his life. At Cambridge, Oppenheimer's intensity led him to near-disaster when he deliberately left an apple, poisoned with laboratory chemicals, on his tutor's desk. The tutor didn't eat the apple – but Oppenheimer's place at Cambridge was threatened, maintained only on the condition that he see a psychiatrist. The psychiatrist diagnosed psychosis but then wrote Oppenheimer off, saying that treatment would be useless.

Instead, Oppenheimer went on to work with a host of world-renowned physicists, from Max Born to Albert Einstein and Bertrand Russell, and was one of the most highly regarded theoretical physicists in the country himself by the 1920s and 30s, despite his troubled personal life.

How did Oppenheimer become known as the 'father of the atomic bomb'?

Oppenheimer was chosen by President Franklin D. Roosevelt to head up the secretive 'Manhattan Project' laboratory in Los Alamos, New Mexico during World War II. On 16 July 1945, with a team of accomplished scientists, Oppenheimer launched the world’s first atomic bomb test in the Jornada del Muerto desert in New Mexico. The blast, codenamed 'Trinity', outshone the sun: with a force matching 21 kilotonnes of TNT, the detonation was the largest ever seen, and created a shockwave that was felt 100 miles away. It was then that he became known for having uttered the now-famous phrase, "I am become Death, the shatterer of worlds" – a quote from Hindu scripture, the Bhagavad Gita.

For the rest of his life, Oppenheimer seemed to wrestle with his involvement in creating the atomic bomb. He co-established what would eventually, in 1960, become the World Academy of Art and Science, because he was increasingly concerned about the potential danger that scientific inventions could pose to humanity.

Whatever guilt Oppenheimer felt was apparently complicated, however, as the scientist seemed to relinquish any responsibility for the 1945 atomic bombings of Hiroshima and Nagasaki in Japan, at the end of World War II. "I carry no weight on my conscience," he claimed in 1961, according to The New York Times. "Our work has changed the conditions in which men live, but the use made of these changes is the problem of governments, not of scientists."

What about Oppenheimer's personal life?

In 1936, Oppenheimer began dating Jean Tatlock (played by Florence Pugh in the film), the daughter of a Berkeley literature professor and a student at Stanford, who was 10 years his junior. They parted after three years and, in August 1939, Oppenheimer met Katherine 'Kitty' Puening (Emily Blunt), a radical Berkeley student and former Communist Party member. She was married at the time, but got divorced when she found out she was pregnant with Oppenheimer's baby; the pair married in 1940 and had two children.

During their marriage, Oppenheimer rekindled his relationship with Tatlock, who tragically died by suicide in January, 1944, leaving Oppenheimer deeply grieved. She was often thought of as the love of his life.

What happened to Oppenheimer?

Life changed dramatically for Oppenheimer during the McCarthy era, when he was one of many Americans accused of being a Communist Party sympathiser because of his political views and relationships, The New York Times says.

Oppenheimer had also been speaking widely about the dangers of nuclear warfare. In 1954, he was brought before the Atomic Energy Commission and lost his security clearance at the hearing. It was later revealed that the FBI had bugged Oppenheimer’s home throughout his tenure at Berkeley. Along with wife Puening and mistress Tatlock, Oppenheimer’s brother and a close friend were both also Communist Party members, though the scientist himself was never an official member.

After the hearing, Oppenheimer reportedly struggled to reconcile his beliefs with reality, and spent the rest of his life trying to grapple with the implications of his scientific work. Oppenheimer died due to throat cancer, following years of smoking, on 18 February 1967 in New Jersey, at the age of 62. Decades later, his influence on the world can still be felt – and this latest exploration of his life and work has reignited interest in this polarising figure once more.
